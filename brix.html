Brix

Brix (°Bx) measures the sugar content of a solution — one degree Brix equals one gram of sucrose per 100 grams of solution. Calculate how much sugar to add to raise Brix, or how much liquid to add to lower it.

How it works

  1. 01
    Raising Brix — add sugar. Given a solution of weight W at current Brix B₀, the sugar needed to reach target B₁ is:
    S = W × (B₁ − B₀) / (100 − B₁)
  2. 02
    Lowering Brix — add liquid. To dilute a solution down to a target Brix, the liquid to add is:
    L = W × (B₀ − B₁) / B₁
    This assumes the diluent is pure water (0 °Bx). For diluting with another liquid (e.g. juice), use its Brix as the current Brix of a separate calculation.
  3. 03
    Measure first. If your liquid already contains sugar — juice, wine, liqueur — measure its Brix with a refractometer before entering it. For plain water or neutral spirits, use 0 °Bx.
  4. 04
    Verify after adjusting. Once at room temperature, confirm with your refractometer. Most refractometers assume 20 °C — temperature affects readings.

Reference Brix values

  • Simple syrup 1:1 (equal weight) — ~50 °Bx
  • Rich simple syrup 2:1 — ~67 °Bx
  • Grenadine (typical) — 55–65 °Bx
  • Fresh orange juice — 10–13 °Bx
  • Fresh lemon juice — 6–9 °Bx
  • Dry white wine — 0–2 °Bx
  • Port / dessert wine — 6–12 °Bx
  • Supersaturation limit (sucrose, 20 °C) — ~67 °Bx
