Brix Sugar Addition

Brix (°Bx) measures the sugar content of a solution — one degree Brix equals one gram of sucrose per 100 grams of solution. Use this calculator to find exactly how much sugar to add to bring any liquid to a target Brix level, whether you're dialling in a house syrup, a liqueur base, or a fruit cordial.

How it works

  1. 01
    The formula. Brix is defined as grams of sucrose per 100 g of solution. Given a solution of known weight W at current Brix B₀, the sugar mass to add to reach target Brix B₁ is:
    S = W × (B₁ − B₀) / (100 − B₁)
    The final solution weighs W + S grams, and its Brix is exactly B₁.
  2. 02
    Measure first. If your liquid already contains sugar (juice, wine, liqueur), measure its current Brix with a refractometer before entering it here. For plain water or neutral spirits, use 0 °Bx.
  3. 03
    Add sugar gradually. Add the calculated sugar to your liquid and stir or heat gently until fully dissolved. Sucrose dissolves faster in warm liquid.
  4. 04
    Verify. Once cooled to room temperature, confirm with a refractometer. Temperature affects readings — most refractometers assume 20 °C.

Reference Brix values

  • Simple syrup 1:1 (equal weight) — ~50 °Bx
  • Rich simple syrup 2:1 — ~67 °Bx
  • Grenadine (typical) — 55–65 °Bx
  • Fresh orange juice — 10–13 °Bx
  • Fresh lemon juice — 6–9 °Bx
  • Dry white wine — 0–2 °Bx (most alcohol fermented out)
  • Port / dessert wine — 6–12 °Bx
  • Supersaturation limit (sucrose, 20 °C) — ~67 °Bx
